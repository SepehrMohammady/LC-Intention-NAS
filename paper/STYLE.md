# Writing style guard — run every draft section against this

Compiled 2026-07-07 from the two articles the team shared (GLBGPT "How to
tell if something was written by ChatGPT"; Grammarly "Common AI words").
Search the manuscript for every item below; each hit is replaced or gets an
explicit justification.

## A. Word blocklist (case-insensitive search)

delve, leverage, robust, seamless(ly), pivotal, foster, underscore, showcase,
realm, tapestry, harness, illuminate, facilitate, bolster, streamline,
crucial, revolutionize, innovative, cutting-edge, game-changing,
transformative, moreover, furthermore, additionally, arguably, notably,
landscape, ever-evolving, comprehensive, holistic, myriad, plethora,
paradigm, navigate, embark, unveil, elevate, testament, meticulous,
intricate, nuanced, groundbreaking, unprecedented, significant(ly) [unless
statistical significance is meant and tested].

## B. Phrase blocklist

"in today's …", "in the ever-evolving landscape of", "it is important to
note that", "it should be noted that", "in conclusion", "overall", "that
being said", "at its core", "to put it simply", "this underscores the
importance of", "a key takeaway is", "from a broader perspective",
"generally speaking", "broadly speaking", "tends to", "to some extent",
"shed light on", "plays a pivotal/crucial role in", "seamless integration",
"scalable solution", "serves as a testament to", "not only X but also Y".

## C. Structural tells

- Rule-of-three triads ("clear, concise, and compelling").
- "not only … but also" correlatives.
- Em-dash overuse — budget: at most 1–2 per page.
- Formulaic openers/closers; every paragraph ending in a mini-summary.
- Symmetrical paragraph lengths, uniform sentence rhythm.
- Stacked hedges ("generally speaking, it typically tends to").
- Generalized claims without citation, number, table or figure reference.

## D. Ten rules for our prose (IEEE style)

1. Plain verbs: use, examine, show — not leverage, delve into, showcase.
2. Quantify instead of intensify: "reduces latency by 23% (Table III)", never
   "significantly enhances performance".
3. No throat-clearing; state the point directly.
4. Introduction opens with the concrete technical problem and measurable
   stakes.
5. Every claim anchored: citation [n], dataset name, Eq./Fig./Table ref.
6. Vary sentence and paragraph length; triads only when exactly three real
   items exist.
7. Em-dashes: ≤2 per page.
8. Hedge once with one calibrated modal (may, suggests); never stack.
9. Active voice with concrete agents ("We prune…", "Section IV evaluates…");
   passive only where the agent is irrelevant.
10. Split "not only/but also" into two direct statements; transitions carry
    content ("Because the encoder is frozen, …") or are dropped.
