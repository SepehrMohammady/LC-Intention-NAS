Lesson 02

The data: what is a Time Series, and what does ours look like? 🎞️

Before building any model, we need to know what it will be looking at: a 5-second "numeric movie" of how the car is doing.

The story 📖

Imagine that at every moment of a drive we take a numeric snapshot of the car: what was the speed? How far was the steering wheel turned? How far was the car from the lane line? Now, if we line these numeric snapshots up one after another, we get a "numeric movie". That numeric movie is called a time series.

Each time, our model sees only a short clip of this movie — as if it looked at just the last 50 frames — and it has to guess what happens next. That short clip is called a window.

More precisely 🔬

Each input sample (one window) is really a table:

50
time steps (timestep) per window
31
signals (feature) per step
10 Hz
recording rate (10 snapshots per second)
5 s
length of each window (50 × 0.1 seconds)

So each sample has shape (50, 31): 50 rows of time and 31 columns of signals. The data also comes to us already prepared and normalized by the team — meaning all the signals were put on a comparable scale (like writing every subject's grade out of 20) so the network can learn more easily.

Where did this data come from? 🎮

The dataset's official name is the Lane Change Intention Recognition Dataset. The data was collected in the CARLA driving simulator: 50 real human drivers drove in the simulator, and the car's signals were recorded at 10 Hz (10 times per second). That is why our 50-step window covers exactly 5 seconds of driving. (The name DMIR is just our project's internal codename, not the dataset's name.)

Splitting the data (splits) 📚

To keep the model's exam honest, the data comes in three parts — just like studying for school: train is the practice workbook (the model learns from it), validation is the mid-term quizzes (we use it to tune the model and pick the best one), and test is the final exam, held only once at the very end. If we keep tuning the model against test, we have effectively leaked the exam answers!

And for all three of our tasks, the split sizes are:

Some good news: in classification, all three classes (none, left, right) have exactly the same number of samples — this is called balanced. That is great, because the model cannot score cheap points with the "always predict the biggest class" trick.

Odd things we found in the data 🕵️

This is what real research looks like: before any modeling, we put the data under a magnifying glass, and two important findings turned up:

⚠️ Two genuine surprises in our data

1. Giant values only in test: a few rare samples in the test split hold values up to 5 million (5×10⁶) — on the signal pairs (12, 13) and (14, 15) — while the largest value in train is about 140! We later proved, by fingerprinting the channels, that these values sit exactly in the curvatureDx channel pair (the rate of change of path curvature): this signal is built from a division, and when the denominator gets close to zero, the result explodes. Our fix: we clamp the val/test values to the train range (clipping) and leave the train data untouched.

Why clipping rather than deleting those samples outright? Because test must remain the same fixed, shared exam so that comparing methods and records stays fair. Deleting a sample changes the exam; clipping keeps the sample and merely pins the out-of-range value to the familiar boundary of train.

🔎 A fresh clue about the "why": the data team told us some drivers crashed in the simulator, and their data was kept on purpose. The test reports (DMIR Test Reports.xlsx) confirm this: for instance, User34 had four collisions and two crashes, and User1 had five collisions and one crash. A crash means a sudden jump or reset in the car's position — and a signal built from a "rate of change" (like curvatureDx) explodes at that very moment. This is a plausible hypothesis, not a proof, because with the current materials we cannot say which driver each exploded window belongs to; but it also fits the fact that the giant values appear only in test. Either way, our fix is the same clipping.

2. Feature number 7 is always zero (in the classification data) — a completely silent signal that gives the model essentially no information.

💡 The big lesson: always inspect the data with your own eyes before modeling. Had we gone straight to the model, these flaws would have quietly corrupted the results.
