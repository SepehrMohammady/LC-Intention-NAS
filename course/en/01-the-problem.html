Lesson 01

The problem: how can a car tell the driver is about to change lanes? 🚗

Before changing lanes, a driver leaves small traces in the car's signals — our job is to find those traces, a few seconds before the maneuver itself.

The story 📖

When someone is about to change lanes, they give off plenty of small cues well before the main move: they turn the wheel bit by bit, adjust their speed, glance at the mirror, drift closer to or farther from the car in front. People read these cues without thinking — when a motorcyclist starts weaving next to you, you can "feel" they are about to cut across, without being able to say exactly how you knew.

The question this project asks: can a computer program, watching those same cues, predict a lane change a few seconds before it happens? If it can, the car has time to warn the driver or prepare itself — and those few seconds can be worth a great deal.

More precisely 🔬

Modern cars are packed with sensors; dozens of signals are recorded at every instant: speed, steering angle, distance to the lane marking, distance to the surrounding cars, and so on. Together these signals form a multivariate time series: several parallel signals that all change over time — a bit like several ECG traces recorded side by side, simultaneously, each telling its own story.

The interesting part is that the lane-change cues — those very "traces" — show up in these signals a few seconds in advance. The figure below is a simple sketch of the idea:

With these signals we solve three problems:

1. Classification with three classes — the model must say which state the driver is in right now:

Class codeMeaning
0No lane-change intention
1Intention to change lanes to the right (LCR = Lane Change Right)
2Intention to change lanes to the left (LCL = Lane Change Left)

2. Regression for LCL: how many seconds are left until the left lane change? The answer is a number between 0 and 4 seconds, in steps of 0.1.
3. Regression for LCR: the same question, this time for the right.

Where does this 0-to-4 range come from? From the labels themselves: only windows whose lane change happens within the next few seconds receive a numeric label. At longer horizons no cue has formed yet, so values above 4 seconds are simply never defined.

And one important constraint: why isn't "accuracy alone" enough? Because the answer has to be computed on a microcontroller inside the car itself — within a few milliseconds and a few hundred kilobytes of memory. A big model that is accurate but slow is like a brilliant scientist who needs a week for every answer: useless here. We want accuracy and compactness and speed, all at once.

In our project 🧰

The best published result on this very dataset (the LC-Intention framework) stands at: accuracy of 92% for classification, and RMSE of 0.42 s and 0.44 s for the two regression tasks. And already on day one, our first simple model — a small CNN, with no special tuning — reached 91.5%:
