Lesson 07

NAS: when the computer designs the network itself 🧬

Instead of building the network architecture ourselves by guesswork and trial, we set up an evolutionary competition: a hundred designs compete, the best ones have children, and the old ones retire.

The story 📖 — Evolutionary cooking

Imagine you have a notebook of 100 recipes and you want to reach the best soup in the world. Every day you play this game: you pick 25 recipes at random from the notebook and taste them; the tastiest of those 25 is today's "parent". Now you change its recipe slightly — a new spice, a little more onion — rather than writing one from scratch. You cook the new recipe, write down its score, and add it to the notebook.

And now the strange rule of the game: the oldest recipe in the notebook retires — even if it was once the champion! Why? Because if the old champion stayed forever, it alone would become the parent every day and the notebook would fill up with its copies; no fresh idea would ever get a chance to grow. But when everyone eventually retires, a good recipe survives only through its "children" — and the notebook stays fresh and diverse. After hundreds of days, the notebook is full of excellent, different recipes.

More precisely 🔬

Step one: the search space

Before any search can start, we have to say "what we are allowed to build". This is called the search space: the set of all possible designs. In our project this space consists of 1D CNN networks: how many layers? Of what type (regular Conv1D, depthwise, or the 1x1 we met in Lesson 04)? What kernel size (3 to 7)? How many channels per layer? pooling or not? And at the end, a small dense head for the final answer.

Each combination of these choices is a different network — and the number of combinations is astronomical. Trying them all is impossible; so we need a clever searcher.

Step two: aging evolution

Our algorithm is called aging evolution, and it is exactly the cooking game — only instead of recipes, we have network architectures:

1. We keep a population (population) of architectures — say, 100.
2. Each round, we draw a random sample — say, 25.
3. The best of that sample becomes the parent (a small tournament, not the whole population).
4. The parent mutates (mutation): one small change, like adding or removing a layer, or widening one (more channels). This kind of structural edit is also called a morphism.
5. The child is trained and joins the population with its score.
6. The oldest member of the population is removed — not the worst!

The golden detail is step 6. If we always removed the "worst", an early lucky winner could stay in the population forever, keep becoming the parent, and fill the population with copies of itself. By removing the oldest, even the champion has an expiry date: its good ideas survive only through its children, and the search never stops exploring.

How do we know who is "oldest"? (especially in round one!) 🧓

A fair question: in round one, when everyone is a newcomer, who is the oldest? The answer lies in how the population is built. In the µNAS code, the population is a queue (queue), not a jumbled bag:

1. Even the initial population is not built in one go: random architectures are created and trained one at a time, and each one is appended to the back of the queue in the order it becomes ready. So from the very first moment, the entry order is fully defined: "oldest" means the front of the queue — the first member that entered.
2. Removal is just as simple: whenever the population grows past the cap (say, 100), the member at the front of the queue is removed — without looking at its score. In the code it really is this one line: population.pop(0).

So "age" has nothing to do with quality; it is purely entry order. This very simplicity is what separates aging evolution from the classic genetic algorithm (removing the weakest): here nobody, not even the champion, lives longer than the "lifetime of the queue".

The families of NAS algorithms 🧭 — and why we picked evolution

Aging evolution is not the only way to search for architectures. There are four main families, and each offers a different bargain:

FamilyHow it worksCostWeak spot
Reinforcement learning (RL)A controller RNN proposes architectures, validation accuracy is its reward, and it learns via policy gradient to propose better onesAstronomicalNeeds thousands of full trainings
Gradient-based (DARTS)Keeps all possible operations side by side in one supernetwork (supernet) with soft softmax weights and lets the gradient itself make the choiceCheapMemory-hungry; sometimes collapses onto skip-connections
Evolutionary (aging evolution)Population + mutation + retiring the oldest — this lessonModerateEvery child must be trained separately
Random (random search)Builds random architectures and scores them — the baseline of every comparisonDepends on the budgetLearns nothing from the past

Why did µNAS choose aging evolution? Because hard resource ceilings — flash memory, peak RAM, number of MACs — bolt onto its fitness very cleanly: a child that blows the budget is penalized right there, with no need for the constraint to be differentiable or for any extra trick. The full story of these constraints is the next lesson.

Step three: there is no single "best" — the Pareto front

We have two goals at once, and they fight each other: low error and small size (so the model fits in a microcontroller). A model that is both smaller and more accurate than another one "beats" it. But very often that is not the case: one is smaller and the other more accurate — neither is the absolute winner. The set of models that nobody can beat on both criteria at once forms a "trade-off frontier": the Pareto front.

That is why the output of NAS is not one model but a "showcase" of optimal trade-offs — and we pick from this showcase depending on the target board. Exactly how we measure "smallness" without the hardware, and which ceilings we hand to the algorithm, is the story of the next lesson (µNAS).

Then we ran a real short search: 25 rounds, each child trained for only 20 epochs. The result was 31 models on the Pareto front. We evaluated the best of them separately on the test set — and the published record (the Transformer of the IEEE SPL 2026 paper) is already broken:

Two notes of scientific honesty. First, these numbers were not handed to us by the search itself. Throughout its run, the search compares candidates by their validation score hundreds of times and picks the best; this repeated selection makes the best validation score slightly optimistic and biased — just like an exam question gradually leaking out, this time to the algorithm. That is why our short search reported an MAE of about 0.11 on validation, while the number that can actually be cited is 0.287: following our standing rule, we loaded the saved model separately with verify_models.py and re-evaluated it on the test drivers (who were never part of training or selection). Second, that was only the "short" search.

Two necessary cautions for this table: first, the published number is a single combined TTLC for both directions, not a separate figure for LCL; so the first column of the LCL row is an aligned comparison, not an exact head-to-head. Second, the internal reference was obtained with a different training protocol and thresholding, so that column is a soft comparison too. We state both openly so the numbers do not look bigger than they really are.

One note so the numbers do not get mixed up: the "31 models" you saw above belonged to that short demonstration search. The full 150-round searches delivered larger Pareto fronts: 33 models for classification, 29 for the no-turn-signal version, 32 for LCR and 33 for LCL (the rmse re-runs that saved every model: 90 and 82).

And the two models in the first rows of the table — the best (model_aaaabl, 84k parameters) and the tiny one (model_aaaaat, about 8k parameters) — are both members of the same 33-model classification front. The tiny model is not a pruned or compressed version of the big one; it is a different architecture that the search itself found, because with save_criteria=pareto the whole accuracy-size frontier is saved, not just a single winner.

A lovely unexpected finding: the network's shape followed the nature of the task. The search allowed parallel branches for both kinds of task, yet the classification models came out nearly linear and the regression models grew multiple branches (LCR two branches, LCL three) — nobody designed this; the search discovered it on its own.

The cost of the search ⏱️ — about one GPU-night

All four full 150-round searches ran in a single overnight queue on one laptop RTX 5070 and finished the next day via chunked resume (chunked resume) (LOGBOOK, July 8 and 9, 2026). The individual duration of each search was not recorded, so we honestly say just this: about one GPU-night for all four searches — not a more precise number that we do not have.

For a sense of scale, the famous numbers from the literature: NASNet with reinforcement learning (Zoph & Le) took about 2000 GPU-days, AmoebaNet with evolution (Real et al., 2019) about 3150 GPU-days, and gradient-based DARTS (Liu et al., 2019) about 1 to 4 GPU-days. A necessary caution: those were entirely different problems and hardware; these numbers are only for context, not a head-to-head comparison.

Real engineering 🔧 — the war with a memory leak

Our first search kept crashing suddenly after about 40 candidates. Why? TensorFlow was keeping the old models — each around 200 MB — in memory until RAM ran out. The fix: release the session after every candidate. The lesson is simple but important: long-running machine-learning jobs need memory hygiene (memory hygiene) — otherwise they die halfway through the third night.
