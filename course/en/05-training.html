Lesson 05

Training: how the network learns 🚴

The network learns by trial and error: make a guess, see how bad it was, nudge the weights a little — and repeat that loop thousands of times.

The story 📖

Remember how you learned to ride a bike? Nobody ever became a cyclist by reading a book! You get on, you wobble, you fall. Every fall is a painful but useful message: "not like that!" And after each mistake your body adjusts itself a little — lean a bit more to the left, turn the handlebars a bit more gently — until, bit by bit, the falls become rarer and shorter.

A neural network learns in exactly the same way. Each fall is the loss: a number that says how bad the network's guess was. And those tiny bodily adjustments are the job of the optimizer: after every mistake, it nudges the network's weights a little in the right direction. There is one subtle point, too: if you correct yourself too hard after every mistake, you fall off the other side! And if you correct too little, summer will end before you learn to ride. The size of that correction is called the learning rate.

More precisely 🔬

First, the loss: a single number that says "how badly you guessed". It is computed differently for each kind of problem:

• For regression (how many seconds until the lane change?) we use MAE (Mean Absolute Error): the average of the absolute errors, in seconds. For example, if the network says "2.0 seconds" and the correct answer is "2.3 seconds", the error for that sample is 0.3 seconds; averaging these errors over all samples gives the MAE. Because its unit is seconds, it is easy to interpret.
• For classification (none / right / left) we use cross-entropy: the network assigns each class a probability; if it gives the correct class a high probability the loss is small, and if it is confidently wrong, the penalty is very heavy.

Now the learning itself: picture the loss as a valley, with the network's weights defining where a ball currently sits on it. Mathematics tells us the slope of the ground (called the gradient), and we roll the ball downhill, step by step, until it reaches the bottom of the valley — the lowest loss. This method is called gradient descent:

Who takes these steps? The optimizer. The most famous one is Adam (and its improved version, AdamW): like an experienced coach, it works out for each weight individually how far and in which direction to push it, and it keeps a bit of "movement memory" so it does not get lost on winding paths.

Two more words you will hear a lot:

• batch: instead of updating the weights after every single sample (noisy) or after the whole dataset (very slow), we feed the samples in batches. We use batches of 256 windows: the loss is averaged over each batch and one step is taken.
• epoch: one full pass through all the train data. Like one complete bike-practice session in the park; several epochs are usually needed.

And one last trick: early stopping. After every epoch, we test the model on the validation data (drivers who were not in the practice set). If several epochs in a row — that count is called the patience — bring no improvement, we cut training short and return to the best weights we had seen.
