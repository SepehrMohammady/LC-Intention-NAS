Lesson 06

Validation and Overfitting: why the network must never see the exam answers 🕵️

If a student has already seen the exam questions, their perfect score means nothing — and exactly the same is true for a neural network.

The story 📖

Picture two students. The first has memorized the solutions manual word for word; in class exercises he always gets full marks. But on exam day, the teacher writes one brand-new question, and the first student freezes — because he had memorized the answers, not the method. The second student memorized less but understood the rule; a new question holds no terror for her.

A neural network can turn into that first student too: instead of learning the real pattern, it memorizes irrelevant details of the training data. This is called overfitting. The only way to find out whether the model has "understood" or merely "memorized" is to never show it the exam questions while teaching.

More precisely 🔬

That is why we split the data into three separate sets — and in our project the split is by driver, not by individual windows:

SetRoleDrivers
trainLessons and practice — the model learns from these only36
validationMock exam — for decisions during training (like early stopping)7
testFinal exam — once, at the very end, only to report the result7

Now overfitting becomes visible to the naked eye: the model's error on the training data (train loss) keeps falling, because memorizing never stops. But its error on data it has never seen (validation loss) first goes down and, past a certain point, turns back up — precisely the moment when "understanding" ends and "memorizing" begins:

Early stopping is the "anti-memorization guard": after every epoch, we measure the model's error on the validation set. If it fails to improve for several epochs in a row (in our project: patience = 10), we cut training short and roll back to the best saved version. Amusingly, although our baseline model was allowed to train for 60 epochs, it found its best state at epoch 3 — and the whole training took just 27 seconds.

True story 1: an exam with unfamiliar drivers 🚗

Our data was collected from 50 human drivers in the CARLA simulator. Every driver has habits of their own: one turns the wheel gently, another overtakes aggressively. If windows from one and the same driver appear both in training and in the exam, the model can memorize that driver's style instead of understanding "lane-change intention" — and the exam loses all validity.

The fix: split by driver. Users 5, 8, 10, 12, 16, 19, 27 appear only in validation, users 2, 7, 13, 18, 25, 31, 36 only in test, and the remaining 36 drivers only in train:

And because in science "trust me" is not enough, we proved it: we matched the raw windows from each driver's sessions against the prepared training windows, one by one, using the correlation coefficient (correlation) — with matches at r > 0.9999 — and saw that the test drivers appear in test and nowhere else. Our exam really is an exam.

First, the basics: what exactly is correlation? 📐

The Pearson correlation coefficient (Pearson r) is a number between −1 and +1 that says how similarly two sequences of numbers rise and fall: +1 means perfectly in step, 0 means unrelated, −1 means mirror opposites. Two of its properties were golden for our job:

1. It ignores scale: if a signal is normalized (multiplied by a constant or shifted by one), its r with the raw version stays exactly 1. That is why we could match the raw windows from the drivers' original files against the normalized windows in the pickle — without ever knowing the normalization formula!
2. Getting it by chance is virtually impossible: two unrelated 50-sample windows might give an r around 0.3 or even 0.8, but r > 0.9999 over 50 points means "this is exactly the same window". So when a window from driver 7 finds its r > 0.9999 partner only in test and never in train, we know for certain that driver 7 was not in training.

Why didn't we use k-fold cross-validation? 🤔

A smart question: on small datasets, instead of one fixed validation set, people usually split the data into k parts and train k times (each part takes a turn as the exam), so all of the data serves both learning and evaluation. We deliberately chose not to, for three reasons:

1. Head-to-head comparison: the published record and the team's reference results are all reported on this exact fixed split of drivers. If we change the split, our numbers are no longer comparable to theirs — and the whole claim of "we improved" becomes meaningless.
2. Cost inside NAS: our search trains hundreds of candidates. With k=7 that means multiplying by seven several days and nights of GPU time — for each of the four searches.
3. The data is not that small: our scarcity is not at the "window" level (we have 94 thousand train windows); it is at the "driver" level (50 people). And the driver-based split measures exactly that.

Honestly: for the final model (not the search), a driver-based cross-validation could show that the result is not sensitive to the particular choice of the 7 test drivers. We have recorded this as a possible supplementary experiment in the paper notes.

True story 2: cheating with the turn signal 💡

Now for a sneakier kind of cheating: imagine an exam sheet where the answer is written in the corner of every question! In our data, two of the 31 channels are the turn signal (turn indicator / blinker) state. Drivers usually signal before changing lanes — so these channels practically give away the "announced answer". This problem is called a label leak.

How did we know the turn signal was suspicious? (No, we did not test all 31 signals one by one!) 🔍

The brute-force way would have been to build 31 models, one signal at a time — or worse, to try every combination (2³¹, more than two billion of them!). The real way was four cheap steps:

1. First we recovered the channel names: the pickle columns were unnamed; with the same correlation trick (matching against the drivers' raw files, which do carry column names) we found that channels 28 and 29 are the left and right turn signals.
2. Driving knowledge: the turn signal is the only signal a driver uses to formally announce their intention — prime suspect, before running a single experiment.
3. One simple count (not even a model!): in what percentage of each class's windows is the turn signal on? Answer: 92% in LCR and 71% in LCL versus only 6.5% in "no intention". That tiny table exposed the leak.
4. Only then, two targeted experiments: a "turn-signal-only" model (how far does the leaked answer alone take you?) and a full "no-turn-signal" search (how far can you get without cheating?).

The methodology lesson: first think cheap (naming, domain knowledge, counting), then experiment expensive — and spend the experiment only on a specific hypothesis.

The results of those experiments:

81.5%
Accuracy from the turn signal channels alone — with no other signal at all!
91.5%
Accuracy of our baseline model (at the time) with all 31 channels
6.5%
Turn signal on in "no intention" windows

In other words, a "model" that only reads the turn signal, with no intelligence whatsoever, gets a good grade on the classification exam — but that is not intention prediction; it is reading an answer the driver announced themselves.

The good news: the regression problem (how many seconds until the lane change?) is clean. The turn signal only says "a lane change is coming", but never when. Compare:

What does ablation mean? 🧪

The word comes from surgery: "removing" a part. In machine learning, an ablation study means deliberately remove one piece of the system, retrain from scratch, and measure the drop — so the true contribution of that piece becomes clear. Crucial detail: the model must be retrained; if we merely zero out an input of the old model, the model is caught off guard and we overestimate the drop.

Our triplet (final results, after the full NAS search):

ExperimentAccuracyWhat does it measure?
All 31 channels92.1%The ceiling with everything available
Without the two turn signal channels91.1%Real "intelligence" — no leaked answer
Only the two turn signal channels81.5%The share of "reading the answer" alone

How to read it: the turn signal alone reaches 81.5% (a real leak!) yet removing it costs the full model only one percentage point — meaning our model performs almost the same without cheating. Without the ablation, neither of those two statements would have been defensible.

Our remedy is twofold. First, our paper leads with regression and real execution on hardware, because no cheating is possible there. Second, for classification we report exactly this ablation — so the reader sees precisely how much of the accuracy is "intelligence" and how much is "reading the answer". That is scientific honesty: we do not hide the dataset's flaw; we measure it and write it up transparently.

In our project 🧰

• Split by driver: 36 / 7 / 7 users for train / validation / test; we proved its correctness by window matching (r > 0.9999).
• early stopping with patience = 10; our baseline model found its best state at epoch 3 (out of a cap of 60).
• We accept no number without re-checking: we re-evaluate every saved model ourselves on the test set with verify_models.py.
• The turn signal leak (label leak) is not hidden in the paper: the classification results with and without the turn signal are both reported.
