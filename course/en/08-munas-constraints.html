Lesson 08

µNAS and Constraints: Fitting a Brain into a Tiny Chip 🎒

Architecture search only makes sense if every candidate model fits the chip's budget first — and that can be measured without the chip itself.

The story 📖

Imagine you have just one small backpack for a one-day hike. You face three serious constraints: space inside the pack (how much gear you can keep unpacked and within reach at any moment), total load weight (how much you can carry), and your walking speed (the more chores along the way, the later you arrive).

Here is the clever part: you don't have to climb to the summit to test each packing arrangement! With a tape measure and a scale, you check everything at home, before setting off. µNAS does exactly the same with every candidate model: before the model ever runs on the chip, it works out on paper how much working memory it needs, how many bytes its weights take, and how many multiply-adds it must perform. Pack space = RAM, load weight = model size in flash, and walking speed = the number of multiply-adds.

More precisely 🔬

For every candidate architecture, µNAS computes three "efficiency" metrics with no hardware at all — purely from the network's structure:

1. peak RAM: at run time, each layer's output (activations) must be held somewhere temporarily. The largest instantaneous memory load during execution is the peak RAM, and it has to fit in the chip's SRAM.
2. model size: the sum of the network's weights in bytes, assuming int8 (one byte per weight). These are stored in flash.
3. MACs: the number of multiply-accumulate operations (multiply-accumulate) for one prediction — the speed predictor. This number correlates with real latency on the STM32 at r²=0.975; it is practically a stopwatch, without the board!

Why does this "no hardware needed" property matter so much? Because the search has to evaluate hundreds of architectures. If each one had to be flashed onto the board and timed with a stopwatch, the search would take weeks. With these three paper metrics, scoring a candidate is nearly instant.

Caps and the scoring formula

These caps are defined in a configuration called BoundConfig: error_bound (the largest acceptable error), peak_mem_bound, model_size_bound and mac_bound. Each candidate is then scored with this formula:

fitness = −maxₖ( min(featureₖ / boundₖ, 10) / λₖ )

We took this formula straight from the µNAS code (aging_evolution.py), and it has three pieces:

1. feature/bound: each metric is divided by its cap so they all share one scale: under the cap means less than 1, exceeding the cap means greater than 1.
2. min(…, 10): the ratios are capped at 10 (cap). Why? If a candidate produces an astronomical error (say 500 times the cap), we don't want that number to make the whole scoring meaningless — "really awful" and "catastrophic" both get the same 10.
3. max with a minus sign: the worst metric is the model's score. If a metric exceeds its cap, its ratio grows past 1, wins the max, and wrecks the score: exceeding a cap is always penalized first.

And what are those lambdas? At every parent selection, four fresh random numbers are drawn between 0 and 1. In other words, the algorithm weighs the metrics through a different lens each time: stricter on memory once, stricter on speed the next (dividing by a small λ makes that metric stand out in the max). The result is that the search doesn't get stuck on one fixed trade-off and instead finds a diverse family of good models (Pareto). But because a cap overflow always wins the max, no lens can ever look past a budget violation.

Our actual budget 📏

Our target board is the STM32H7B3I-DK:

280 MHz
Processor — Arm Cortex-M7
1184 KB
On-chip SRAM (working memory)
2 MB
flash (permanent storage)

And the caps we set in BoundConfig — deliberately much tighter than the board's capacity, to keep the models compact:

MetricOur capWhy this number?
peak RAM (activations)256 KBFar below the board's SRAM; leaves room for the rest of the application
model size (int8)256 KBFar below the board's flash; we also have our eye on smaller boards
MACs2,000,000A stand-in for speed (correlation r²=0.975 with real latency)

Why so strict? Because we also have a stretch goal: the low-cost STM32F401 board with only 96 KB of RAM and 512 KB of flash. In this very lab's published paper, their Transformer model did not fit in this board's RAM at all, and their 1DCNN took 40.8 ms. And the gamble paid off — by now it is not "luck" anymore, it is measurement: our tiny model ran on this same F401 in 4.376 ms and took only 7.2% of the board's flash; the 441k-parameter reference model does not fit on this board at all (3.4 times the entire flash!). In our project 🧰

• The search runs on the ELIOS lab's version of µNAS (on TensorFlow); the caps in the table above are written directly into BoundConfig.
• This budget has already delivered: a 72,729-parameter candidate reached val MAE 0.41 after only 3 epochs, and the best model of one short search, after independent evaluation on the test set, reached MAE 0.287 — better than the published record (0.298).
• We accept no number from the search itself: we re-evaluate every saved model ourselves with verify_models.py on the test set, and every run is logged in logs/experiments.jsonl.
