Lesson 11

Paper writing: how results turn into a paper 📝

We have spent months running experiments and collecting good numbers; now we have to turn all of it into a document a few pages long that convinces the strictest reviewers in the world.

The story 📖

Science is like a big courtroom. We have a claim: “Our small model predicts the time of a lane change more accurately than the previous record, and it also runs on a real chip.” The paper is our case file: every claim we write in it must have its evidence right there — a table, a chart, a reproducible experiment.

The judges of this court are the reviewers: anonymous scientists who read the case file and hunt for the smallest hole. If a single number appears without evidence, or a weakness is hidden, the case comes back. That is why our rule is simple: no claim without evidence, and no weakness hidden.

More precisely 🔬

IEEE papers almost always share a fixed skeleton. Once you know this skeleton, you can read any paper quickly:

SectionWhat does it say, in one sentence?
AbstractThe whole story in a few sentences: what we did and what numbers we got.
IntroductionWhy this problem matters and exactly what new thing we add.
Related WorkWhat others have done so far and where the gap for our work is.
MethodThe complete recipe: precise enough that anyone could repeat the work.
ExperimentsThe evidence: tables and figures that prove every single claim.
ConclusionThe wrap-up: what we learned and what the next step is.

Every number must be traceable 🔎

When a reviewer asks “where did this 0.287 come from?”, we must be able to answer within seconds. That is why we have kept three habits from day one: every run is recorded with all its details (settings, runtime environment, results) in logs/experiments.jsonl; the reason behind every decision is written in LOGBOOK.md; and after every change to the paper text (paper/main.tex), the PDF is rebuilt so the text and the numbers never drift apart. We also have one golden rule: no number enters the paper unless we have re-measured the saved model ourselves with verify_models.py on the test data.

And the case's main claim already has its evidence — our best NAS-searched model (which we independently verified on test ourselves) against the published record:

The second pillar of the case is real execution on hardware — all measured, not estimated: 92.08% accuracy versus 91.69% for the reference model, with 9.2× the speed and 5.2× less memory on the same board with the same toolchain; and on the cheap F401 board, the reference model does not fit at all while our tiny model runs (Lesson 10).

Peer review: the strict inspectors 🕵️

Reviewers are anonymous, they do not do pleasantries, and this is precisely their job: finding weaknesses. The best defense? State the weakness ourselves, first. The real example in our project is the turn-signal leak: the two turn signal channels alone — with no other intelligence at all — reach 81.5% accuracy on test in the classification task. Why? Because the turn signal almost always gives the game away:

The good news: the regression task is clean. Using the turn signal alone, MAE only reaches 0.72 and 0.90 seconds, while our baseline model gets 0.318 and 0.333 — meaning the model truly learns from driver behavior, not from the turn signal. That is why our paper moves forward with regression and the real on-board deployment, and for classification it openly reports a with/without turn signal comparison experiment (called an ablation). A reviewer who sees that we have been the strictest inspectors of our own work will also trust the rest of the numbers more.

The turn-signal leak is not our only act of honesty; we also state two other weaknesses explicitly: first, some internal RMSE targets (the team's reference results) are still unbeaten, and we do not hide it; second, int16×8 quantization is not deployable with the ST toolchain (it silently falls back to float32), so we report it only as an offline accuracy bound, not as a deployed configuration.

Which journal? ⚖️

We have two main options, and the choice is genuinely hard:

JournalRankPage limitIFFirst response (weeks)
IEEE Signal Processing LettersQ144.6≈6
IEEE Internet of Things JournalQ188.7≈7

SPL is the very journal where the current record (this lab's 2026 work) was published — so our comparison will be seen exactly where it belongs. But its 4-page limit is tough: our story (NAS + quantization + real on-board execution + the turn signal honesty experiment) barely fits into 4 pages, and things would have to be sacrificed. IoT-J, with 8 pages, gives room to breathe, has a high IF (8.7), and its first response also takes about 7 weeks — but its audience is slightly different. Now that the final results (the searches and the on-board measurements) are in, this decision will be finalized with the supervisor: the size of the story determines the size of the journal.

The style guard: numbers instead of adjectives ✍️

There is one amusing danger, too: text that reads as “robot-written”. Some words have been repeated so often in AI-generated text that reviewers raise an eyebrow the moment they see them. That is why paper/STYLE.md keeps a blocklist: delve, leverage, robust, seamless, and their friends.

The golden rule of style 🏅

Do not hand the reviewer adjectives; hand them numbers and tables.

❌ “Our model outperforms prior work in a seamless and amazing way.”
✅ “Our model's MAE is 0.287 seconds versus 0.298 for the previous record (Table III).”

The first sentence is debatable; the second is checkable. In science, only the second one counts.

In our project 🧰

• logs/experiments.jsonl: every run is recorded with settings + environment + results — no number ever gets lost.
• LOGBOOK.md: the reason behind every decision, for the day someone asks “why this way?”
• paper/main.tex: the paper draft; after every change, the PDF is rebuilt.
• paper/STYLE.md: the list of banned words and the “numbers instead of adjectives” rule.
• verify_models.py: no number enters the paper without independent verification on test.
• The honesty rule: the turn signal leak is openly reported with a with/without experiment.
• The journal choice (SPL or IoT-J) is made after the full searches are finished.
