Lesson 03

Neural Network in Plain Language 🧠

A guessing machine that starts out answering completely at random, but nudges itself a little after every mistake.

The story 📖

Imagine you want to teach a friend to recognize musical instruments by ear, but you are not allowed to state any rules; you can't say "a guitar sounds stringy" or "a piano sounds percussive." All you can do is play songs for them thousands of times and say "that was a guitar," "that was a piano." Little by little, your friend's brain finds the patterns on its own — without ever being able to say exactly how.

A neural network does the same thing with numbers: a guessing machine that starts out answering completely at random, but corrects itself a little after every mistake. After millions of tiny corrections, its guesses become excellent.

More precisely 🔬

The network is built from layers of neurons. Each neuron is a very simple creature: it takes a few input numbers, multiplies each one by a weight, adds them all up, and passes the result through a simple non-linear function called an activation. The most famous activation is ReLU, which has just one rule: "if it's negative, make it zero; otherwise leave it alone."

Think of the weights as the volume knobs on a mixing desk: each knob decides how loudly one input is heard in the final answer. "Learning" means finding the right setting for exactly these knobs.

The learning loop: four steps, millions of times 🔁

So how does the network find the right setting for the weights? With a simple loop that repeats over and over:

1. The network makes a guess for one sample (this stage is called the forward pass);
2. A function called the loss measures how bad the guess was — like a teacher who only hands out marks;
3. The backpropagation algorithm works out how much each weight was to blame for the error;
4. The optimizer (such as AdamW, the one we use) nudges each weight a little in the right direction.

The important point: none of these corrections works miracles on its own; each time, the weights get only "a little" better. The magic of a neural network is in the repetition — millions of tiny corrections that add up.

An epoch is made of "steps" 👣

We saw that one full pass over all the train data is called an epoch. But the network doesn't look at the data one sample at a time, and it doesn't look at it all at once either; the data is split into small groups called batches, and the weights are updated once after each batch. So:

Steps per epoch = number of samples ÷ batch size

For example, with 94,620 samples and a batch size of 256, each epoch takes about 370 steps. Why batches? If we update after every single sample (batch = 1), each step reflects the opinion of just one sample, and the learning path becomes jittery and slow; if we update after the whole dataset, each step is very expensive. A medium batch gives a steadier average and also keeps the GPU better fed — in our project, batch = 256.

What does the network's output look like? 🎯

• For classification, the network outputs three numbers (one per class), and the largest one is the network's guess.
• For regression, the output is a single number: how many seconds until the lane change.

But how do we measure the "error"? Two kinds of loss 📏

We said the loss tells us how bad a guess was — but "being bad" is not the same thing for these two kinds of output, so we need two different formulas:

• For regression (a continuous output, like "2.3 seconds") we use MAE (mean absolute error): the distance between the guess and the right answer. If the network says "2.0" and the answer is "2.3", this sample's error is 0.3 seconds — and since the unit is seconds, the meaning is immediately clear.
• For classification (one probability per class) we use cross-entropy: this formula measures the network's confidence in the correct class, and heavily penalizes answers that are "confidently wrong." "2.0 instead of 2.3" is a distance, not a class — which is why MAE is of no use here.

So the type of loss must match the problem. More detail on training and these two losses comes in Lesson 05; here you only needed to know why we have two separate formulas.

In our project 🧰

• Our baseline model is a small 1D CNN (Lesson 04 explains it in detail) in src/models/baseline.py.
• The learning loop is implemented in src/train.py: AdamW + early stopping (if several epochs in a row bring no improvement, stop).
• First run: only 27 seconds of training on the GPU and 91.5% accuracy on the test data — recorded in logs/experiments.jsonl.
