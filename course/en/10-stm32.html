Lesson 10

STM32: Running the Model on Real Hardware 🧩

The moment of truth: the model we spent months building on a big computer now has to live inside a chip the size of a fingernail.

The story 📖

So far, our model has lived on a powerful computer with a graphics card. But its real home is somewhere else: a microcontroller — a complete computer on a chip that costs a few euros. Everything is right there: the processor, the RAM, and the permanent Flash storage; no heavyweight operating system, no fan, no screen. You power it on and, within a fraction of a second, it is running its one and only program.

Why are cars full of these chips? Because they are cheap, they use very little power, and they work in real time — they always answer within a predictable amount of time; for brakes, steering, and airbags, that reliability matters more than anything else. The price of this simplicity? Memory and speed thousands of times below a laptop's. Our model has to fit into this tiny home.

More precisely 🔬

Our target board is the STM32H7B3I-DK: an Arm Cortex-M7 processor with these specs:

280 MHz
CPU speed (Cortex-M7)
1184 KB
internal SRAM (working memory)
2 MB
Flash memory (program and weights)

To get a feel for what these numbers mean: a laptop with 16 GB RAM has roughly ten thousand times the working memory of this board! That is why we never draw the two on one chart — the board's bar would simply disappear. 😅 This gap is exactly what gives our whole project its meaning (a small model, NAS with memory ceilings, quantization).

From model file to chip: the toolchain 🔗

The output of the previous lesson was an INT8 TFLite file — the compressed model with 8-bit weights. We hand this file to ST's official tool: ST Edge AI (its command-line name is stedgeai). It does three things for us:

• analyze: with no hardware at all, it reports how much flash the model takes, how much RAM it needs, and how many MACC (multiply-accumulate) operations it performs. So we know whether the model fits before ever touching a board!
• validate: checks numerical correctness — does the converted model give the same answers as the reference model?
• ST Edge AI Developer Cloud: a free, remote "board farm" — it runs the model on real boards and measures actual latency (the time per prediction), without a single board on our desk.

Papers in this field usually report four numbers alongside accuracy — and we will do the same:

MetricWhat does it mean?
flash (KB)The size of the model plus program in permanent storage — it must fit in the board's Flash.
peak RAM (KB)The largest amount of scratch memory needed mid-run — it must stay below the SRAM.
latency (ms)How many milliseconds does one prediction take? — measured on the real board.
MACsThe number of multiply-accumulate operations; computed without hardware, and a very good predictor of latency (in our STM32 measurements, r² = 0.975 correlation with real time).

A real-world comparison 📏

The published paper from this very lab (IEEE SPL 2026) deployed its models without quantization (plain float32). On the main board that was fine; but on the cheaper STM32F401 (just 84 MHz, 96 KB RAM, and 512 KB flash) the story got interesting:

Model (from the published paper)On the STM32F401latency
Transformer (about 54k parameters)Did not fit in RAM! ❌—
1DCNNRan ✅40.8 ms
XGBoostRan ✅1.15 ms

This table says exactly why this lesson matters: good accuracy is not enough. A model that does not fit in memory effectively does not exist; and a model that is too slow reacts too late to give a timely lane-change warning. Our goal: our NAS models, in int8, must both fit and be fast — even on a budget board like the F401.

In our project 🧰

• Target board: STM32H7B3I-DK; the more ambitious target: that same low-power STM32F401.
• We took the NAS search ceilings straight from this board's budget: at most 256 KB of peak memory (peak RAM), at most 256 KB of int8 model size, and at most 2,000,000 MACs — so every model that comes out of the search is pre-sized for this chip.
• Measurement plan: first stedgeai analyze for the flash/RAM/MACC report without hardware, then the ST Edge AI Developer Cloud for real latency on the board.

Status of this stage ✅ — everything measured!

This table is no longer an estimate: every number was measured on the real STM32H7B3I-DK board through the ST Edge AI Developer Cloud (version 4.0.1, balanced optimization, float32 format — the same setup as the reference paper). We also benchmarked the team's 441k-parameter reference model with the exact same settings, so the comparison is apples-to-apples:

In plain terms: our model is at once more accurate (92.08 versus 91.69), 9.2× faster, and 5.2× smaller than the reference model; and the tiny version, at under one millisecond, is 42× faster while giving up only 0.4 points of accuracy. The int8 operating point was measured too: 1.885 ms and 104 KB for classification — but with a 5.2-point accuracy drop, whose why and how Lesson 09 explained.

And on the tiny F401 board? A knockout 🥊

Our tiny model ran on the NUCLEO-F401RE (that same cheap 84 MHz board) in 4.376 ms, taking just 7.2% of the board's flash. The reference model? It does not get onto the board at all: 1729 KB of weights against a total of 512 KB of flash — 3.4× larger than the board's entire memory, and no setting can save it. This is no longer "several times better"; it is to be or not to be: our searched models open the door to a hardware class that the reference model stays locked out of.

A fun number for intuition: this same tiny model spends about 7 cycles per multiply-accumulate on the M7 and about 12 on the M4 — on processors that finish a multiply-accumulate in a single cycle! At this scale, most of the time goes into moving data around and per-operation overhead, not the math itself. That is why MACs is excellent for comparing whole models (r²=0.975) but not reliable for predicting the time of individual layers.
