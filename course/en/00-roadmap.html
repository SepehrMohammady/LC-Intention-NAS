Lesson 00

Roadmap: what are we going to build? 🗺️

A tiny but clever assistant that guesses the driver's intention a few seconds before a lane change.

The story 📖

Imagine a very small, very fast assistant sitting next to the driver of a car. Its job is exactly one thing: a few seconds before the driver changes lanes, guess that they are about to do it. If the assistant catches it early, the car can raise a warning or prepare itself, and many collisions can be avoided.

Here is the problem: this assistant has to live inside a very small, cheap chip (called a microcontroller — like an STM32), not inside a big computer. A small chip means little memory and limited speed. So our assistant has to be both smart and tiny.

More precisely 🔬

This assistant is called a model (a neural network). Its input is the car's signals over time (a time series), and it does three jobs:

Our three problems:

1. Classification: does the driver intend to change lanes or not? Three cases: none, right (LCR = Lane Change Right), left (LCL = Lane Change Left).
2. Regression for LCL: how many seconds until they move left? (0 to 4 seconds, in steps of 0.1)
3. Regression for LCR: the same question for the right.

One subtle point: the label "how many seconds until the maneuver?" is only meaningful for windows that actually end in that same direction. That is why LCL and LCR are two different datasets, each with its own separate model.

And instead of designing the network architecture ourselves by trial and error, we use NAS (Neural Architecture Search): an algorithm that tries thousands of designs on its own and finds the best one — under the condition that it fits in the microcontroller's memory (the µNAS approach). Why do we put this condition inside the search from the very start? Because if we first build the most accurate model and then try to shrink it to fit (pruning or compression), accuracy usually drops; a constrained search only ever looks among models that fit, and optimizes accuracy within that same space.

The end goal 🎯

Break a scientific record! The best published result on this data (the IEEE SPL 2026 paper from this very lab) and the team's internal reference results are these:

Where we stand today 🏁 (this is already done!)

• NAS is finished: our best model, with 84k parameters, reached 92.08% accuracy (target: 92%), and in regression the published record (RMSE 0.51) was broken with RMSE 0.447. (The internal targets of 0.42/0.44 have not been beaten yet — we say so honestly!)
• The models were measured on the real board: our model is 9.2× faster and 5.2× smaller than the reference model — with higher accuracy.
• On the cheap F401 board, the reference model does not fit at all, while our tiny model runs in 4.4 ms. Details in Lessons 07 to 10.
